  • What is captioning?

    We use “captioning” as a blanket term for the readable text of anything being spoken. People are most familiar with captioning on television broadcasts. CaptionAccess provides non-broadcast captioning for live events and captioning for pre-recorded media. Word-for-word captioning is often called CART (Communication Access Realtime Translation). We also offer a condensed form of live captioning, sometimes referred to as meaning-for-meaning translation. Try both, see which you prefer. Either way, we’ll make you well connected.

  • Who would want captioning?

    Deaf and hard-of-hearing people use captioning to understand what is being said in any life situation – business meetings, school classes, webinars, public events, teleconferences, church services...the works. Use of captioning as a means to provide communication access increases each year and will continue to grow as more people learn about it as a means of access.
    But captioning isn’t just for deaf and hard-of-hearing people, it benefits everyone. Many people with normal hearing need captioning to understand what is being said on television in noisy places, such as sports bars and health clubs. People who learn English as a second language often understand English text better than speech. Significantly, captioning creates a transcript that can serve as a record of webinars, business meetings, and classroom lectures. Transcripts also can be posted online, giving the website’s SEO a boost.

  • Why captioning instead of sign language interpreting?

    According to Johns Hopkins University, there are 48 million deaf and hard-of-hearing people in the United States. Only about 500,000 use American Sign Language as a main mode of communication and typically choose sign language interpreters for their communication access. The rest of those 48 million use various combinations of amplified hearing, speechreading, assistive listening technology, and text (captioning!) to understand what people are saying. This group benefits the most from captioning--a whopping one fifth of all Americans.

  • What is the difference between live captioning and post-production captioning?

    Captioning that happens live as a speaker is talking is referred to as realtime captioning. The text version of what is being said appears almost instantaneously on the screen of a PC, laptop, tablet, projector, or smartphone. Post-production, or pre-recorded, captioning is the process of adding text to existing video or audio. Examples of this include captioned videos and webcasts posted online or archived on websites as on-demand events. Post-production work involves time-coding the text so the captioning syncs exactly with the speaker’s words. Just as importantly, CaptionAccess editors make sure the words themselves are exactly right.

  • Why can’t we just use speech recognition technologies?

    Yes, people ask this question all the time. If you can do a web search or text message by voice on a cellphone, why can’t you use automatic speech recognition (ASR) to caption a business meeting? Because you can’t. Although amazing strides have been made in ASR technology, ASR has yet to achieve the accuracy where it can dependably be used for communication access. ASR depends largely on familiarity with the speaker’s voice and the specific terminology used. The error rate of ASR can be quite high making it unreadable--even comically so--in whole or in part. Check out automatic captioning on YouTube and judge for yourself.

  • CaptionAccess works remotely. Why?

    Technology allows CaptionAccess to provide remote captioning anywhere there is a good Internet connection, which is almost everywhere these days. With remote captioning, customers save money by not having to pay for captioners to travel, park, and sometimes stay in hotels to work on site. What’s more, many of our customers appreciate not having an extra person in the room calling attention to the fact that they’re receiving special services. Remote captioning also gives us greater flexibility to provide short-notice services and deal with last-minute schedule changes. We don’t have to be in the neighborhood to do the job.

  • How is remote captioning done?

    The remote captioner listens to the speakers via teleconferencing or an Internet audio source, such as Skype or GoToMeeting. The captioner then creates the text using a stenotype machine, re-voicing technology, or advanced abbreviation software. That text is transmitted through cyberspace and appears on an Internet-capable display device. It’s pretty simple actually, and highly effective.

  • What do I need on my end to get captioning?

    For classes, business meetings, and many other events, you will need a stable Internet connection, a microphone, an audio link, and a device to display the captioning. Having a good-quality mic and audio link are especially important because captioning quality depends on the ability of the captioner to hear the discussion. We can recommend a mic and audio setup appropriate for your situation. And our free demo also can come in handy.

  • Does captioning meet compliance requirements of the Americans with Disabilities Act?

    State and federal civil rights laws, such as the ADA, prohibit discrimination against individuals who require special accommodations. Captioning fulfills ADA requirements for communication access. In determining how government regulations apply to your situation, don’t hesitate to contact us.
